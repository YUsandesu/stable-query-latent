The Illusion of Night City: A Critical Deconstruction of Cyberpunk 2077's Mechanics and Narrative

After the overwhelming hype bubble burst, Cyberpunk 2077 exposed its truest colors: a crude sandbox that masks compromised design with flashy neon lights and barely keeps running with heavy patching, alongside a fatalistic tragedy that uses "destiny" to disguise the player's utter helplessness.

I. A Compromised and Piecemeal System: The Shackled "Survival Law"

The game's progression system is essentially an elaborate deception. In the prologue, the game offers you three seemingly grand "Lifepaths"—Nomad, Street Kid, and Corpo. Yet, apart from providing a brief, ten-minute prologue that has zero impact on the grand scheme of things and some subsequent inconsequential dialogue options, they do absolutely nothing to change your fundamental nature as a corporate/street "cog in the machine."

The so-called five core attributes feel more like a clumsy imitation of traditional RPG clichés:

Body simplifies combat into aesthetic-free brute brawls and crude shotgun blasts;

Reflexes, despite introducing flashy katana-slashing and time-dilation, turns combat into unrealistic visual light pollution;

Technical Ability, under the guise of "cyberware enhancement," in reality becomes a numerical trash bin that forces players to stack stats just for armor and cyberware capacity;

Intelligence reduces netrunning offenses and defenses to a boring minigame of staring at enemies from afar to kill them, completely stripping away the tension of technological warfare;

Cool nominally focuses on stealth and assassination, but in the face of the game's bug-ridden, brain-dead AI, these stealth mechanics feel redundant and clumsy.

The Cyberware system, praised as the soul of the game, is actually a form of artificial "decision paralysis" created by restricting player freedom. Operating systems force you into a binary, mutually exclusive choice between a Cyberdeck (playing as a boring netrunner) and a Sandevistan or Berserk component (playing as a tedious time-stopping/damage-immune berserker). Double jumps or Optical Camo do manage to marginally improve vertical exploration, but they simultaneously expose the embarrassment of Night City’s massive invisible walls and unfinished textures. Meanwhile, the seemingly badass Mantis Blades, Gorilla Arms, Monowire, and Projectile Launch System are, in actual combat, nothing more than redundant melee alternatives stuffed into your flesh.

Even the so-called three firearm archetypes are rife with mechanical corner-cutting:

Power Weapons: The ricochet mechanic is virtually useless in chaotic real combat; very few players can actually utilize deflections to hit enemies hiding behind cover.

Tech Weapons: Charged shots that penetrate walls completely destroy the cover logic of level design, turning combat into a dull experience akin to using a wall-hack.

Smart Weapons: Micro-guided homing bullets are essentially "official aimbots" that completely strip away the player's basic satisfaction of aiming and shooting.

II. A Tragedy of Fate and Nihilism: A Narrative Stripped of Player Agency

If the game mechanics are mediocre compromises, the main storyline of Cyberpunk 2077 is a futile struggle stripped of player autonomy, filled with helplessness and irony.

It all begins with that absurd and fatal heist. The protagonist, V—a low-life mercenary in Night City blinded by greed and the desire for fame—stupidly takes on a high-risk, high-reward job far beyond their capabilities with their best friend Jackie Welles: infiltrating Arasaka Corporation's luxury hotel to steal the experimental Relic chip. The operation fails disastrously as expected when they witness the patricidal tragedy of Yorinobu Arasaka strangling his own father, Saburo Arasaka, and framing an assassin. In their frantic escape, Jackie is mortally wounded. To protect the fragile chip, V is forced to slot the Relic into their own brain, only to be immediately betrayed and shot in the head by the fixer, Dexter DeShawn.

Yet, death does not grant V a clean break. The Relic chip uses nanotechnology to repair V's brain, only to usher in a far more cruel torture: it activates the digital consciousness (Engram) of Johnny Silverhand, the arrogant, paranoid terrorist/rockerboy who blew up Arasaka Tower 50 years ago. This hubristic soul begins to ruthlessly erode and overwrite V's remaining memories like terminal cancer. The ripperdoc Viktor's diagnosis makes it brutally clear: V's body has only a few weeks left, and they will eventually be entirely overwritten by a dead man's ghost.

To survive, V begins to scurry around like a headless chicken, begging for scraps among Night City's various factions:

You kidnap the selfish chip developer Anders Hellman, only to find no cure;

You are forced to beg the cold and sinister Voodoo Boys for help, trying to contact the rogue AI Alt Cunningham beyond the Blackwall, only to end up as a mere pawn in their chess game.

Throughout your frantic search, you meet the nomad Panam Palmer, the detective River Ward, and the bar girl Judy Alvarez, seemingly forging deep bonds. Yet in your slowly rotting mind, you are forced to compromise with the parasite Johnny Silverhand, descending into a pathetic, Stockholm-syndrome-like symbiosis.

When all struggles lead to a dead-end, Arasaka Tower's server, Mikoshi, becomes your sole remaining hope. No matter which path you choose:

Whether you risk everything with the Aldecaldos, letting countless innocent nomads die for your sake (The Star ending);

Whether you surrender and compromise with Hanako Arasaka, selling your soul and dignity to the corporation for a hollow, living-dead existence (The Devil ending);

Whether you hand over control of your body to Johnny, watching your old friend Rogue die for you in Arasaka Tower once more (The Sun / Temperance ending);

Or even go in guns blazing, alone, in a suicidal charge to find a brief moment of tragic glory (Don't Fear the Reaper / Hidden ending).

In the digital ruins of Mikoshi, the AI Alt Cunningham will always slap you with a harsh reality: your destiny has already been sealed, and your body has degraded too far. Even after stripping Johnny's consciousness away, your body is left with a mere six-month lifespan. If you refuse, you must surrender your physical form entirely to Johnny, reducing yourself to a spectral stream of data wandering the vast, empty expanse of cyberspace.

Whether you return to a dying shell with only six months of hopeless existence, or yield everything to a ghost from the past—Cyberpunk 2077 uses this quintessential cyberpunk dark humor to teach players one final lesson: in this hell constructed of neon and steel, all your struggles and rebellions have, from the very beginning, been nothing but a slow-motion suicide.
