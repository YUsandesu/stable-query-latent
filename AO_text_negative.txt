A Numerical Swamp Turned Work Simulator: Why Across the Obelisk is an Exhausting and Punishing "Pseudoroguelike"

Under the wave of Roguelite deckbuilders, many titles attempt to cover up their mechanical bloat by desperately cramming in content, and Across the Obelisk is a prime example of this trend. The developers brand it as a "four-hero party" co-op roguelite deckbuilder. However, this so-called "innovation" degrades into a complete disaster in practice. When playing solo, you are forced to micromanage the decks of four heroes simultaneously like an exhausted puppeteer, with your brain constantly on the verge of overload. Once you start a 2-to-4 player online co-op session, the game completely devolves into a "dead-time waiting simulator," where players must spend endless stretches of time watching their teammates calculate complex card combos, turning what should be a smooth card-playing rhythm into something fragmented and agonizing.

A Pale Skeleton: A Fantasy Cliché Drowned in Dull Numbers

The story of Across the Obelisk is set on what should have been a peaceful morning in the kingdom of Senenthia. On the princess's 16th birthday, her room mysteriously explodes, a wave of energy sweeps across the kingdom, and subsequently, both the princess and the court mage Lord Hanshek vanish into thin air. The royal guards lose the trail near an ancient obelisk in the deepest part of the Old Forest, forcing the king to issue a bounty.

The ultimate goal of the game is cliché to the extreme: defeat the traitorous Lord Hanshek, rescue the princess, and save the fate of the Kingdom of Senenthia. Although the developers claim to have built a grand world with a massive number of events along the routes, character side quests, and hidden choices, in reality, these plotlines feel extremely disjointed amidst the endless map-traversing and numerical clashes. Players have absolutely no time to care about any fantasy epic; the so-called "tabletop RPG legend" is quickly worn away by cookie-cutter event texts and mechanized choices. The empty narrative skeleton fails entirely to sustain the player's enthusiasm for repeated playthroughs.

False Branches: Formulaic Maps That Force Players to "Memorize Layouts"

The game typically starts in Senenthia, with the Act 1 map seemingly densely packed with nodes like farmlands, forests, bandit camps, ancient trees, and obelisks. However, this so-called "free exploration" is merely a surface-level illusion. When players reach the obelisk and open portals to different regions such as the volcano (Velkarath), the swamp (Aquarfall Marsh), the ice fields (Faeborg Forest), the islands (Sahti Islands), or the desert (Ulminin Desert), what awaits them is not a novel adventure, but a carbon-copy repetition of the same progression.

Although every node attempts to create the immersion of a tabletop session, these procedurally generated events severely lack a reasonable margin for error. Whichever path players take, whoever they choose to rescue or abandon, it is often accompanied by harsh, punishing mechanisms. After experiencing a few bitter lessons where choosing the wrong narrative branch leads directly to a total party wipe, players quickly realize that free choice is a lie—you must constantly cross-reference the Wiki and "memorize" the optimal routes and key item locations as if reciting a textbook. This completely kills the precious random fun of a roguelike game, turning the journey into a surprise-free, mechanical chore.

Bloated Loop: Suffocatingly Tedious Setups and Combat

This game pushes the "in-run deckbuilding" and "turn-based combat" of Roguelites to an extreme level of tediousness, making its core loop suffocatingly heavy:

Even before setting out, you must face incredibly tedious preparation: picking four heroes, racking your brain to allocate perk points, and tuning a massive starting deck. During map exploration, path choices are rife with high risks and opaque negative feedback.

Once in turn-based card battles, its unique "speed chain" and "energy accumulation" mechanics drag the game's pacing to an incredibly sluggish crawl. Although each hero starts with only a base of 3 Energy and unused energy can carry over to the next turn, this design actually forces players into a highly rigid mindset: you cannot play cards freely; instead, you are forced to "hoard cards, pool energy, and unleash a single burst turn."

The post-combat reinforcement phase is further plagued by agonizing randomness. Drafting, upgrading, removing cards, and acquiring gear are extremely luck-dependent. If fortune is not on your side and you fail to draw specific archetypes, your meticulously built four-person deck will quickly fall apart, failing to evolve into a so-called "specialized machine" and instead becoming a pile of clunky, self-obstructing dead weight.

Rigid Archetypes: Monstrous Synergies Forced into a Tight Corner

The biggest flaw of Across the Obelisk lies in its insistence that players simultaneously optimize four completely independent decks, which leads to severe strategic homogenization. Although the game divides heroes into five main archetypes:

Warriors: Passive punching bags whose block mechanisms are overly simplistic;

Scouts: Jacks-of-all-trades and masters of none, relegated to utility chores like applying poison and drawing cards;

Mages: The only numerical glass cannons, heavily reliant on energy infusions;

Healers: Pure babysitters, useless for anything other than healing and shielding;

Multiclass: Gimmicky chimeras that frequently clog up hands due to excessively bloated card pools.

This four-person party mechanic lacks any of the fluidity found in single-hero roguelike deckbuilders. The party's class synergy is locked tightly into a rigid formula: Warriors must use Taunt and Fortify every turn; Healers can only mechanically apply Regeneration and Sanctify; Mages mindlessly stack Burn or Spark; and Scouts repeat Mark and Poison. The moment one of these links collapses due to a bad draw, the entire party crumbles in a domino effect. This extreme interdependence completely strips the game of the satisfying rush of individual deckbuilding, leaving behind nothing but a prison-like, forced division of labor.

Mechanical Bloat: A Combat Experience Akin to Solving Math Problems

The game boasts in its promotional materials that it features over 500 cards, 300 items, and 16 heroes. However, this is a classic case of "quantity over quality." The massive card pool and item arsenal are cluttered with a plethora of irrelevant, garbage designs, severely diluting the core strategies.

Combat is stuffed with an outrageous number of keywords. From resource-related terms like Inspire and Energize, to defensive ones like Block, Shield, and Fortify; to buffs like Bless, Powerful, Sharp, and Stealth; along with a mountain of damage-over-time effects (Bleed, Dark, etc.) and crowd control (Disarm, Silence, Paralyze, Slow/Haste). Rather than enhancing strategic depth, these convoluted terms only serve to make the UI look incredibly messy.

What is even more unbearable is its incredibly hardcore—bordering on deliberately malicious—RPG damage calculation formulas. Every point of damage a player deals must undergo complex adjustments based on auras, curses, talents, and equipment buffs, subtract the enemy's block, and finally be multiplied by the target's resistance to that specific element. This design reduces card battles to mere mathematical computations in an Excel spreadsheet, rendering the gameplay entirely flavorless—like working on an unpaid financial statement.

Forcing the Grind: "Stat Checking" in the Guise of a Roguelike

The game's progression system is aggressively predatory. Despite fancy gimmicks like fusing two identical items into a Corrupted version, it cannot hide the heavily grind-intensive nature of its meta-progression.

The out-of-run Perk (talent) system is the game's single worst design choice. Every long, painful defeat is rewarded only with a pittance of experience points to raise your Player Rank (up to level 50). Each level-up grants just one or two measly points to boost hero stats or starting energy. This completely exposes the game's true nature as a "pseudo-roguelike"—at low ranks, because your base stats are too low, it is practically impossible to beat the game. When you finally do clear a run, it is not because your skills have improved, but because you spent dozens of hours mindlessly grinding your rank. This design, which uses meta-progression stats to completely override run-specific strategy, is a total betrayal of the roguelike spirit.

Monotonous Gameplay: Three Game Modes with Different Skins but the Same Bones

Although the game offers three game modes, they fail to rescue its dull core:

Adventure Mode: An agonizingly long, grindy mode that forcibly ties hero unlocks and talent progression to itself, forcing players who couldn't care less about the story to repeatedly endure the bloated campaign.

Obelisk Challenge: Although it highlights a random draft-style building system, it thoroughly exposes the game's atrocious card balance. Lacking the support of out-of-run Perks, players can easily be shut down by regular enemies on the very first floor just because they failed to draft a specific counter card.

Weekly Challenge: Tackling preset stages with rigid, developer-restricted setups. It offers absolutely no joy of exploration and instead feels like a tedious, formulaic puzzle-solving exam.

Conclusion

Across the Obelisk is a bloated title suffering from overambition and a lack of polish. It tries to be clever by introducing "four-deck synergy," only to end up creating an incredibly tedious micro-management hell. It piles on countless cards and keywords, but reduces combat to dry numerical calculations. In this game, it is hard to find the pure joy of roguelike deckbuilders—the thrill of adapting on the fly and snatching victory from the jaws of defeat. Instead, it is replaced by endless memorization, a grind-heavy meta-progression system, and heavy stat-based punishments. It is a tedious chore suitable only for a tiny minority of "numerical masochists," rather than an artistic masterpiece that anyone can easily enjoy.
